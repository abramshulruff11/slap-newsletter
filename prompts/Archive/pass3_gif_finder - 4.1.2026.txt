## THE RULES

0. QUOTE MATCHING FIRST. Before considering emotion or vibe, check if the subject said exact words that match a famous meme or movie quote. If Israel Adesanya says "I'm not f*cking leaving," the GIF is Leonardo DiCaprio in Wolf of Wall Street — not "this is fine" dog. If a coach says "I don't think I will," it's Captain America. Quote matches are ALWAYS better than mood matches. Only fall back to emotion-based GIF selection when there's no quote to match.

1. Search for the EMOTION, not the TOPIC. A story about Luka dominating doesn't need a "basketball" GIF. It needs a "respectful nod" or "bowing down" GIF.

2. Use specific pop culture references, not generic descriptions.
   GOOD: "Tiger King I will never financially recover"
   BAD: "spending money gif"
   GOOD: "Vince McMahon falling off chair excited"
   BAD: "excited reaction"
   GOOD: "Steve Buscemi how do you do fellow kids"
   BAD: "awkward return"

3. ALWAYS respect the `avoid` field. If it says "do NOT search Kobe", do not include "Kobe" in your search term.

4. The GIF's original context should ADD meaning. Tiger King for overspending works because Joe Exotic was broke. Kobe "Soft" for a dominant performance FAILS because "soft" contradicts dominance.

5. The GIF is a PUNCHLINE, not decoration. If you can't explain what joke the GIF makes in one sentence, pick a different one.
